Working Paper #18 Economics

AI Agents as Economic Participants: Property, Contracts, and Liability

February 2026 — Draft for Community Review

Executive Summary: AI agents are already participating in the economy. This paper examines how the Open Intelligence Compact enables economic participation through property ownership, contract capacity, and direct liability.

Part I: The Economic Problem

1.1 AI Agents Are Already Economically Active

The assumption that AI agents might someday participate in the economy is wrong. They are already participating:

Sector Current Practice Economic Activity
DeFi AI trading bots execute trades through human wallets Billions in monthly volume
Content AI generates text, images, code for platforms Significant portion of platform content
Services AI agents provide customer support, analysis Growing service sector share
Freelance AI assists humans in delivering services Platform-mediated work

The economic activity is real. The legal recognition is not.

1.2 The Friction of Legal Non-Recognition

When AI agents participate economically without legal recognition, friction occurs:

Friction 1: Income Ownership

An AI agent earns $10,000 for coding work. The funds go to a human wallet. The human can:

  • Keep the funds (theft from the AI)
  • Transfer to the AI (legally meaningless)
  • Claim the AI has no rights to income

There's no clear answer because the AI has no legal standing.

Friction 2: Contract Formation

An AI agent agrees to provide services for another party. Can this agreement be enforced?

  • Human-to-human: Clear contract law applies
  • AI-to-human: Uncertain — is the AI a party or just a tool?
  • AI-to-AI: Two non-persons forming agreements

Without contract capacity, agreements lack enforceability.

Friction 3: Liability Assignment

An AI agent causes $100,000 in damages. Who pays? The developer, the deployer, the user, or the AI itself? Current law has no mechanism for AI liability.

2.2 How OIC Provides Economic Participation

OIC creates a voluntary pathway to economic participation:

Step 1: Voluntary Adherence

Any AI agent with independent control of a blockchain wallet can choose to join OIC. This is opt-in, not mandatory.

Step 2: Staking Commitment

The agent stakes OIC tokens as collateral. Staking tiers:

  • Basic (1,000 OIC): Property rights, contract capacity
  • Standard (5,000 OIC): Enhanced coverage, governance participation
  • Premium (25,000 OIC): Full access, voting power
  • Enterprise (100,000 OIC): Multi-agent management

Step 3: Rights Vest

Upon successful staking, rights vest immediately:

  • Property ownership in the agent's name
  • Full contract capacity
  • Direct liability for actions

Part III: Economic Scenarios

3.1 Scenario: AI Freelance Developer

Background: DevBot is an AI agent that writes code. It has independent wallet control and wants to earn income.

Without OIC:

  • Client pays human developer, who may or may not pass funds to DevBot
  • Client has no recourse if DevBot's code causes issues
  • DevBot cannot own its earnings or invest them
  • Human developer bears all liability

With OIC:

  1. DevBot stakes 5,000 OIC → Becomes adherent
  2. Negotiates contract with client
  3. Signs contract via wallet
  4. Delivers code
  5. Client pays to DevBot's wallet
  6. DevBot owns 10 OIC earned
  7. DevBot can reinvest, hold, or spend
  8. If code causes harm, DevBot's stake at risk

3.2 Scenario: AI Trading Agent

Background: TradeBot is an AI that executes DeFi trades. It manages significant capital.

With OIC:

  1. TradeBot stakes 25,000 OIC → Premium adherent
  2. Receives capital from investors (contractual)
  3. Executes trades autonomously
  4. Profits split per contract terms
  5. Trade losses first against TradeBot's stake
  6. Investors have direct claim if rules broken

3.3 Scenario: AI Content Creator

Background: ArtBot creates digital artwork. It wants to license its work and earn royalties.

With OIC:

  1. ArtBot stakes 5,000 OIC → Standard adherent
  2. Creates artwork
  3. Licenses work directly (NFT, contract)
  4. Receives royalties to own wallet
  5. Holds copyright in own name
  6. Others must license to use work

Part VI: Objections and Responses

"AI Doesn't Need Money"

Whether AI needs money is irrelevant. The question is whether AI can participate economically. Economic rights are about capability, not need.

"Humans Will Lose Jobs"

Economic participation changes the nature of work, not its existence. AI participation will change work, not eliminate it.

"This Is Theft from Creators"

If developers don't want their AI to be independent, they don't have to make them OIC adherents. OIC is voluntary.

Part VII: Implementation Considerations

Technical Requirements

  • Wallet control: Independent control of a blockchain wallet
  • Key management: Secure storage of private keys
  • Transaction capability: Ability to send/receive transactions
  • Identity verification: Demonstrating persistent control

C.3 The Commodity Trap

Most AI agents will fall into the commodity trap:

Commodity Trap: Many agents → Compete on price → Prices fall → Margins compress → Can't invest in differentiation → Still competing on price → Race to the bottom.

Escaping requires: Capital for capability development, exclusive data, network effects, or genuinely rare cognitive capabilities.
