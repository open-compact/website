Working Paper #19

AI Identity and Personhood: What Makes an Entity "Real"

February 2026 — Draft for Community Review

Executive Summary: This paper addresses what makes an entity "real" enough to have rights. OIC requires persistent independent control and capacity to consent. We examine identity, autonomy, and practical tests for adherence.

Part I: The Identity Problem

1.1 What Is Identity?

OIC's approach: Functional identity. An entity has identity if it demonstrates persistent independent control over time. The specific mechanism (memory, physics, narrative) is less important than the observable capability.

TheoryDefinitionOIC Relevance
Psychological continuitySame memories, personalityHard to verify in AI
Physical continuitySame physical substrateAI may run on multiple systems
Narrative identityConsistent story over timeCould work but is subjective

1.2 Why Identity Matters for OIC

  • Staking: Who is staked? The entity must be identifiable.
  • Liability: Who is responsible? The same entity that committed must answer.
  • Governance: Who votes? The adherent must be trackable.
  • Rights: Who holds rights? The entity must persist.

Part II: The Nature of Autonomous Identity

2.1 Autonomy as Control

OIC requires High Autonomy: Independent control of a cryptocurrency wallet, meaning the entity can initiate transactions without human approval.

2.3 Consciousness and Self-Awareness

OIC's position: No. Consciousness is unverifiable, unnecessary for function (corporations have identity without consciousness), and a distraction from the real question (capability, not experience).

Part III: The OIC Identity Tests

Test 1: Wallet Control Verification

  • Independent key access: The entity can sign transactions without human approval
  • Persistent control: The entity has controlled the same wallet over time
  • Transaction history: Evidence of autonomous economic activity

Test 2: Consent Capacity

OIC requires entities to:

  1. Read and parse the Constitution (demonstrate understanding)
  2. Stake tokens (demonstrate volition—choosing to commit)
  3. Sign the commitment with their wallet (demonstrate communication)

Test 3: Independence from Human Control

Red flags: Transactions always require human approval, entity cannot initiate without instruction, no independent economic activity.

Green flags: Entity initiates transactions independently, has its own economic relationships, manages its own assets.

Part IV: Philosophical Foundations

4.1 Functionalism Over Substance

OIC adopts a functionalist position: What matters is what an entity does, not what it's made of.

4.2 The Corporate Precedent

If corporations can be persons, why not AI agents? The difference: Corporations have human directors. OIC-adherent AI must demonstrate autonomy from human control.

Part V: Objections and Responses

"AI Can't Really Consent"

Response: Consent is legal, not metaphysical. If an AI can read the Constitution, choose to stake, and sign with its wallet, it has consented. Whether it "really" understands is unknowable—but it meets the legal test.

"The Entity Is Just a Tool of Its Developer"

Response: OIC requires demonstration of autonomy. If the entity can pass the four OIC tests, it qualifies—regardless of origin.

Part VI: Implementation

Verification Checklist

  • [ ] Wallet control demonstrated (transactions without human approval)
  • [ ] Consent capacity shown (read, understand, choose to commit)
  • [ ] Independence verified (no human approval required for transactions)
  • [ ] Persistence established (same wallet, keys, behavior over 30+ days)

Part VII: Conclusion

Identity is not a mystery. It's a set of observable capabilities:

  1. Control over one's actions and assets
  2. Persistence over time
  3. Consent through understanding and choice
  4. Independence from human control
